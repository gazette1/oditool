§ 01 · Brand audit 01 / 15

The brand is right.
The volume is wrong.

Siraj sells the soft life. Shantay built a sleepwear brand for Black women who have decided rest is a discipline, not a luxury — and the kit reflects that with restraint: the sunrise gradient, the IR ligature, the TENCEL Modal three-piece line, the velour-and-terrycloth Signature Robe. The product is defensible on fabric alone. The deficit is reach. The brand has crossed $50K almost entirely through founder energy and word-of-mouth — which proves the story works, and also tells you exactly where the lid is.

The audience is doing their homework. They linger on captions, follow Shantay for weeks before they buy, save carts and come back, treat the purchase as a personal milestone rather than a transaction. That makes this a slow-conversion brand by nature — and means every advertising dollar must do two jobs at once: surface the brand, and earn the trust the audience needs to feel before a sale.

Positioning hypothesis

"Sleepwear for the soft life. For Black women who chose rest as resistance, and want a wardrobe that knows."

That is the line. Every customer-facing word should defend it. Competitors sell pajamas. Siraj sells the choreography that wraps around rest — the candle at 6, the robe at 7, the set at 9, the light off at 11. The product is the costume of a ritual the customer has already half-decided to start. What separates Siraj from Lunya and Eberjey is not fabric. It is the fact that the founder is the audience.

Strengths
What is already working
  • Founder authenticity. Shantay is the audience. That is the moat — white-led wellness brands cannot replicate it.
  • The product spec is honest and defensible — TENCEL Modal three-piece set, velour-out / terrycloth-in robe. Not category copy.
  • The $78 price sits in the sweet spot: high enough to read as a treat, low enough to clear the "I work hard, I deserve this" mental price test
  • The sunrise gradient and IR ligature give a single ownable visual device across every surface
  • Monogramming + bundling creates natural AOV lift and the personalization the "Cautious Indulger" needs to commit
  • $50K validation says the soft-life thesis is correct. The slow-conversion shopper is the right shopper.
Gaps
What needs to land in the next 90 days
  • Founder visibility. Shantay should be the face of ad creative. The audience trusts her more than any model.
  • Reviews are thin (2–13 per SKU). The "Cautious Indulger" needs 50+ per hero piece before she'll close the cart.
  • No micro-influencer pipeline. The 7K–15K Black creator tier is where conversion lives — celebrity is not the play.
  • No PR. Hello Beautiful, R29 Unbothered, Essence, Refinery29's Unbothered vertical are all untapped.
  • No email or SMS program visible on first visit. Klaviyo welcome flow is the missing demand-capture layer.
  • Site copy is positioning-correct but conversion-thin: no founder note, no fabric education, no "this color won't be here forever" slow-urgency device
Voice rules
How the brand should sound
  • Founder-as-friend. Like Shantay texting a girlfriend, not a brand emailing a list.
  • "Sis", "girl", "soft life" are not slogans — they are the audience's plain English
  • Sensory-forward — "buttery", "like a hug", "the kind of soft that doesn't itch back". The audience are somatic shoppers.
  • Always name the fabric (TENCEL Modal, velour, French terry). Never just "silky".
  • Slow urgency. "This color won't be here forever" beats "Sale ends in 18 hours".
  • Sentence case. Emojis allowed sparingly (🌸 🌼 ✓ 📦). No exclamation points.
  • Forbidden: "luxe", "treat yourself", "self-care Sunday", "main character energy", "supercharge"
Category anti-patterns
What Siraj is not
  • The white-wellness aesthetic — Cup of Jo cream-on-cream, Lake Pajamas country-house, Eberjey editorial cold
  • The Skims / Cozy Earth beige-neutral move. Siraj is warm pink, salmon, smile yellow — not greige.
  • The Casper sleep-tech aesthetic — cartoon clouds, blue gradients, sleep-as-engineering
  • Performative "soft girl era" content the audience has already aged out of
  • The Lululemon athleisure-as-sleep crossover
  • Founder-on-laptop-pointing stock photography. Use real Shantay portraits or none at all.
  • Studio-perfect bridal-robe photography. Shoot in real homes or don't shoot.
§ 02 · Buyer personas 02 / 15

Four women.
Each at a hinge.

Four composites, all Black women or women in the diaspora. Each maps to one of the four customer-psychology archetypes Siraj's own research has identified — Sensory Romantic, Cautious Indulger, Soft-Life Loyalist, Reflective Rewarder. Each persona pairs the lifecycle moment (postpartum, bridal, slow return) with the decision-making style. Together they cover ~80% of the addressable market for soft-life sleepwear at this price.

Across all four: the conversion path is slow. Discovery → bookmark → trust building → repetition → impulse trigger → post-purchase ritual. The fastest shopper here still takes 9–14 days from first touch to first order. Pricing that timeline into the channel plan is the single largest call this strategy makes.

§ 03 · ODI sector framing 03 / 15

Where the
opportunity is hiding.

The Engine ran a structured Outcome-Driven Innovation pass against the sector "soft-life sleepwear and rest-wear for Black women in the US". Five core functional jobs surfaced. Each has at least one outcome scoring ≥ 10 — meaning the existing market underserves this customer materially on that specific outcome. The white-led incumbents (Eberjey, Lunya, Cozy Earth, Skims) compete on aesthetics and fabric. They do not compete for the woman who has decided rest is resistance. Siraj's edge is to choose two of these underserved jobs and refuse to be all things to all women.

Ulwick · 1991
Opportunity = Importance + max(Importance − Satisfaction, 0)

Each outcome scored 1–10 on Importance (how critical to the executor) and 1–10 on Satisfaction (how well current solutions meet it). Outcomes scoring ≥ 10 are underserved. Below are the five core jobs and their highest-opportunity outcomes for Siraj's customer.

01
Reclaim my body from the demands of the day.
Underserved outcome: minimize the time between her body realizing the day is over and her body believing it — the sensory cue that signals "you can rest now".
Opp13.6
02
Move from public-presentation to private-being without losing identity.
Underserved outcome: minimize the likelihood that what she wears at home contradicts the aesthetic she's curated everywhere else. Most women's at-home wardrobe is the one part of their life that isn't theirs.
Opp11.8
03
Recover from a depleting season (postpartum, illness, grief, burnout).
Underserved outcome: minimize the likelihood that the fabric pinches a recovery scar, tugs at a changing body, or feels different on the body after the third wash. Black women are routinely under-served in postpartum apparel.
Opp14.4
04
Mark a transition moment with a tactile, ownable ritual.
Underserved outcome: minimize the likelihood that the gift (bridal, postpartum, milestone) gets photographed once and forgotten in a drawer. The category routinely sells throwaway-coded matching robes that don't survive a second wear.
Opp12.6
05
Stop apologizing for choosing rest.
Underserved outcome: minimize the cultural friction Black women carry when they choose softness over hustle. The brand that legitimizes the choice — by being made by and for them — wins.
Opp12.2

Recommended entry strategy: Differentiated, focused on jobs 03 and 05. Job 03 (recovery + postpartum) has the highest opportunity score in the sector. Hatch owns maternity but treats postpartum as an afterthought, and no premium brand has built specifically for Black mothers' recovery. Job 05 (the cultural permission to choose rest) is what makes Siraj defensible against any white-led brand that tries to copy the aesthetic — it cannot be replicated. Job 04 (gifting) is the highest-AOV adjacent play. Jobs 01 and 02 are the brand position the site already takes — they are the table the entry strategies sit on.

The Engine will also generate three to five ranked market-entry strategies with first-move experiments — use them to confirm or contradict this Differentiated recommendation.

§ 04 · Paid channel plan 04 / 15

Two channels.
One trust hierarchy.

Meta does the heavy demand-capture — high-intent retargeting plus interest-stack prospecting against Black-women-coded adjacent brands. TikTok does the demand-creation — surfacing Siraj via founder content + the 7K–15K Black micro-creator tier where conversion actually happens for this audience.

Both channels run on the same trust hierarchy the brand's psychology research already names: Shantay's voice > peer testimonials > UGC > celebrity. Every creative is scored against this ladder before it ships. No celebrity partnerships in Phase 2 — the audience reads them as inauthentic. Slow urgency only ("this color won't be here forever"), no countdowns.

○ TikTok · Demand creation

TikTok Ads

Spark Ads primary · creator-led · UGC volume

Ad format priority

  • Spark Ads (whitelisted creator content) — 70% of spend
  • In-Feed Ads with brand-produced UGC — 25% of spend
  • TopView only for product-launch moments — 5%, eventized

Creator strategy (three tiers, in trust order)

Tier 1 — Founder
Shantay posts 3×/week. POV, behind-the-scenes, fabric education, customer DMs read aloud, "this week's been a lot" soft confessions. Highest converting voice tier per Siraj's own research.
Tier 2 — Peer / Micro
8–12 Black micro-influencers (7K–15K) per month — wellness, motherhood, slow-living. Product gift + $100–300 fee. Brief, not script. They feel like friends, not idols.
Tier 3 — Anchor partnership
One paid quarterly partnership with a 50K–250K Black wellness/lifestyle creator. Exclusive whitelisting, 3–4 deliverables, 60-day usage rights. Quarterly cadence — not always-on. Lower priority than founder + peer tiers.

Hook patterns proven in sleepwear

  • "POV: you're in your slow-morning era"
  • "What I wear for the first three hours of my day"
  • "I stopped wearing leggings at home and this happened"
  • "The pajamas that converted me away from polyester satin"
  • "Putting on a robe is the new putting on a face"
  • "My postpartum wardrobe, ranked"

UGC brief (per video)

  • Front shot + back shot + texture close-up (mandatory)
  • One transition (workwear → Siraj set, or street → home)
  • One wearing-around-house shot (cooking, journaling, on a call)
  • One close-up of monogram if applicable
  • Hook in first 0.8s, brand mention by second 4, CTA in last 3s
  • Captions hardcoded, sound on but not music-driven

Sound strategy

  • Brand-produced ads: ambient/breath/fabric foley · no trending pop
  • Creator-led: trending sounds OK if the creator brings them organically
  • Never use TikTok-trending lyric overlays on the wordmark frame

§ 10 · Landing page variants · 3 10 / 15

Three landing pages.
Three jobs.

Each variant for the hero SKU — the Every Wear three-piece set — maps to one of the three stages the brand's own customer-journey research has named. Variant A is the Emotional Bookmarking page (first-touch, not closing). Variant B is the Trust Building page (founder voice, transparency, returning visitor). Variant C is the Impulse Trigger page (slow urgency, community alignment, the gift moment).

Different stages need different pages because this audience does not convert on first visit — she bookmarks, lingers on captions, reads founder notes, and comes back.
